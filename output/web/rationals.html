Section 3.4 Rational Numbers: Equivalence and Reducibility

The equation

\begin{equation*} 2x = 1 \end{equation*}

has no solution in \(\Z\text{.}\)

To make division universally possible (excluding division by zero), we introduce the rational numbers:

\begin{equation*} \Q = \left\{ \frac{a}{b} : a,b \in \Z,\ b \neq 0 \right\} \end{equation*}

A rational number is any number that can be expressed as a quotient of two integers.

Subsection 3.4.1 Terminating and Repeating Decimals

Theorem 3.4.1. Terminating Decimals.

A rational number has a terminating decimal expansion if and only if, after reduction to simplest form, its denominator has no prime factors other than \(2\) and \(5\text{.}\)

Example 3.4.2. Terminating Decimals.

\begin{gather*} \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{2^2} = 0.25\\ \frac{1}{25} = \frac{1}{5^2} = 0.04\\ \frac{3}{8} = \frac{3}{2^3} = 0.375 \end{gather*}

Example 3.4.3. Repeating Decimals.

\begin{gather*} \frac{1}{3} = 0.\overline{3} = 0.333\ldots\\ \frac{1}{6} = 0.1\overline{6} = 0.1666\ldots\\ \frac{1}{7} = 0.\overline{142857} \end{gather*}
