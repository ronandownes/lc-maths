Section 3.3 Primes and the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

Definition 3.3.1. Prime Number.

A prime number is a positive integer greater than \(1\) with exactly two positive divisors: \(1\) and itself.

Definition 3.3.2. Composite Number.

A composite number is a positive integer greater than \(1\) that is not prime.

Example 3.3.3. Examples of Primes and Composites.

  • Primes: \(2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13\)

  • \(1\) is neither prime nor composite

  • Composites: \(4=2\times 2\text{,}\) \(9=3 \times 3\text{,}\) \(51=3 \times 17\)

Theorem 3.3.4. Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic.

Every positive integer \(n>1\) can be written as a product of prime numbers, and this factorisation is unique up to the order of the factors.

Example 3.3.5. Prime Factorization.

\begin{equation*} 84 = 2^2 \cdot 3 \cdot 7 \end{equation*}

This factorisation is unique. No other combination of primes multiplies to give \(84\text{.}\)

Tool: Prime Factorisation.

All multiplicative structure arises from primes. Prime factorisation is the key tool for divisibility, simplifying fractions, and finding HCF and LCM.

Subsection 3.3.1 Divisibility and Place Value

Our number system is written in base ten (Hindu-Arabic positional notation). The structure of base ten, combined with prime factorisation, produces divisibility tests.

Theorem 3.3.6. Divisibility by 2.

A number is divisible by \(2\) if its unit digit is \(0, 2, 4, 6,\) or \(8\text{.}\)

Theorem 3.3.7. Divisibility by 3.

A number is divisible by \(3\) if the sum of its digits is divisible by \(3\text{.}\)

Example 3.3.8.

  • \(51\text{:}\) digit sum \(5+1=6\text{,}\) divisible by \(3\)

  • \(57\text{:}\) digit sum \(5+7=12\text{,}\) divisible by \(3\) â†’ \(57=3 \times 19\)

  • \(52\text{:}\) digit sum \(5+2=7\text{,}\) NOT divisible by \(3\)

Subsection 3.3.2 Highest Common Factor and Lowest Common Multiple

Definition 3.3.9. Highest Common Factor.

The highest common factor (HCF) of positive integers \(a\) and \(b\) is the greatest positive integer dividing both \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\)

Definition 3.3.10. Lowest Common Multiple.

The lowest common multiple (LCM) of positive integers \(a\) and \(b\) is the smallest positive integer that is a multiple of both \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\)

Example 3.3.11. Finding HCF and LCM by Fraction Reduction.

Find HCF and LCM of 84 and 60.

Solution.

Form the fraction and reduce completely:

\begin{equation*} \frac{84}{60} = \frac{7}{5} \end{equation*}

The fraction reduced by dividing by \(12\text{,}\) so:

\begin{equation*} \text{HCF}(84,60) = 12 \end{equation*}

Cross-multiply the reduced fraction:

\begin{equation*} 84 \times 5 = 420 \quad \text{or} \quad 60 \times 7 = 420 \end{equation*}

Therefore:

\begin{equation*} \text{LCM}(84,60) = 420 \end{equation*}

Theorem 3.3.12. HCF-LCM Identity.

For positive integers \(a,b\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} ab = \text{HCF}(a,b) \cdot \text{LCM}(a,b) \end{equation*}
